Sovereign   Writers   Subjects   Sound  






Rhythm and harmony are the metronome of subjectivity. (Plato) To read sound is not just to read musical notation, as some modern political theorists surmised(See Jean-Jacques Rousseau). Reading sound is reading landscapes and the amplitudes of space. The spatiality of sound, a tool in the composition of cinema, helps to consider the importance of such things as microphones and PA systems, but also community events and sound installations. So even if John Street writes that a song of resistance is political "in the mere act of singing" (p. 44), everything we hear is already routed through a sonic grid of wires and/or virtual nodes.

Because singing into the analog-digital ocean does not ensure any kind of recognition or reception, sometimes what is needed is noise. Filmmakers like Puhipau and Joan Lander of Nā maka o ka ʻāina amplified the voices of Hawaiʻi in the cries of eviction and political struggle. So it is not just music, but the resonance of sound itself that functions as "a form of reporting" (See Street 2011, p. 48)
