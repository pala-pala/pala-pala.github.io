"[T]he "politics of the people" wrongs policy, because the people is always more or less than itself. It is the power of the one more, the power of anyone, which confuses the right ordering of policy."

-Jacques Rancière, "Politics, Identification and Subjectivization">

There are many ways to count people in the world, often orchestrated across some larger distribution of space. Thongchai Winichakul, in his book Siam Mapped(1997), demonstrated how national space (geographic, discursive, and visual) came to be consolidated in the age of colonial expansion, then reformatted into a range texts that amplify some sense of a "we-self." These days, the projection of space is intimately linked to sphere's of influence where many packets of ourselves are transported into virtual zones of influence. Meanwhile, many actual spaces are going dark.
In a concrete sense, maps provide a context for making people and things count: for instance, protest sites, tourist destinations, red light "entertainment" districts, or fault lines--the more spectacular the "attractions," the brighter the visualization. Counting, says Rancière, is a police logic that makes one thing visible rather than others.
